Visual analytics on an artificial graph

Graph Drawing

In this project, I aimed to improve the representation of an artificial network graph by exploring a 2.5 dimensional perspective.

Year

2018

Category

Masters coursework

Organisation

The University of Sydney

01 — Context


Graph drawing is concerned with the geometrical representation of network graphs. It is motivated by the applications of network graphs in diverse areas such as social network analysis and bioinformatics, where visualising information helps specialists to better understand their data.
The same network graph can be drawn in several ways. As many real-world network graphs are large and complex, metrics relating to the computational efficiency as well as the human readability of a drawing a graph are main concerns in this area of study.

The main components of a network graph are edges and nodes. Usually nodes represent an entity (e.g. a person in a social network), while edges connect nodes representing some relation between them (e.g. an edge representing a friendship between two persons in a social network).

02 — Design


I used yEd and Tulip to draw the graph, using mixed methods of Betweenness Centrality analysis, degree analysis, orthogonal layout and manual editing.

03 — Evaluation


Strengths


As the whole graph is non-planar, the use of an overall 2.5D projection helps the viewer distinguish the connections of the graph. For example node 33 appears as if it is situated above subgraph 1 with its edges pointing downwards to the “surface”. While in subgraph 2, retaining the original spacing gives the illusion of cubes with depth which helps the viewer navigate the edge connections.


We can find interesting observations such as weak spots within the full network. Nodes with both a high betweenness centrality and low degree suggest that their few ties are crucial for the network to flow.

The use of visual variables of node size (for Betweenness Centrality) allow us to see which nodes are passed through the most within the full network. Additionally, node colour highlights the nodes with the most degree. These two variables combined, we can find interesting observations such as weak spots within the full network (e.g. nodes with both a high betweenness centrality and low degree such as node 48 and 53 suggest that their few ties are crucial for the network to flow).

Weaknesses


My solution introduces edge bends which is not ideal, because bends increase the difficulty of our perception to navigate the link between nodes. This is a trade-off for minimising the number of edge crossings - edges that cross over each other introduces ambiguity in which nodes are actually linked to each other. Moreover, the edges with bends mostly have a long length compared to the straight edges. This was a result of retaining a rectangular area of subgraph 1 to mimic the surface of subgraph 2 to ease visualisation in this projection.
Finally, there is variability in the spacing of nodes which is a trade-off to minimise overall edge lengths. The use of space itself can be interpreted to represent similarity based on the relative proximity of nodes in a graph. Future work would involve improving the node spacing of the entire graph and exploration of geometric shapes to minimise edge bends.
