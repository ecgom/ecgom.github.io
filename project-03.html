Social capital & US health outcomes

Data analysis

In this project, I examined the relationship between the prevalence of religious and non-profit establishments and county-level health outcomes in the United States. A better understanding of the social make-up of community and how it affects health, allows for policymakers and local decision makers to be more effective in communicating, providing and nurturing health-enhancing services for their citizens. I used several multiple linear regression models on a social capital dataset (RGF) proposed by Rupasingha, Goetz & Freshwater, and the 2018 County Health Rankings dataset.

01 — Problem & Hypothesis


Social capital is a term to describe the observable aspects of social organisation at the ecological level, such as the density of social establishments and networks or patterns of civic engagement within a community. A place with low social capital is reported to negatively affect community members’ ability to form social ties and gain information, which in turn affects that community’s population health outcomes.

We studied two establishments that influence social capital at the US county level: religion- based and non-profit establishments. We argued that religious establishments and non-profit establishments influence health in different ways. We theorised that community religiosity positively affects a community’s subjective mental and physical health outcomes, however, lack in health-directed programs that target the reduction of premature death. We suspected non-profit organisations are associated with reduced rates of premature death through community and health programs, and in turn improve community member’s perceptions of physical and mental health.

02 — Methods & Results


Three multiple linear regression models were conducted to assess the association between number of religious and non-profit establishments, and health outcomes of premature death, physically unhealthy days and mentally unhealthy days at the county level, while controlling for a county’s median household income, education, and black/white racial segregation rate.
Our findings suggested that counties with higher instances of religious establishments were associated with better self-reported physical and mental health, but increased rates of premature death. Counties with higher instances of non-profit establishments were associated with decreased rates of premature death, but weaker negative associations with self-reported physical and mental health.

03 — Discussion


Religious establishments showed a stronger inverse association to reported average physically and mentally unhealthy days compared to non-profit establishments. A possible interpretation of this is that religious establishments may be a more enriching resource for social ties and norms for community members compared to non-profit organisations. Another interpretation could be that more religious communities may perceive health in a holistic way that encompasses their beliefs and values and thus may respond differently to subjective health questionnaires.

The inverse association between non-profit establishments and years of potential life lost (YPLL) supports our hypothesis that denser areas of non-profits may improve community health outcomes by providing more services that contribute to health factors and/or health directly. It is difficult to determine a causal link, given that the prevalence of non-profit establishments does not necessarily equate to service effectiveness within a county.
